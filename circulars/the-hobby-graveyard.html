The Hobby Graveyard

Nature of the Holding

The Department maintains a classification for objects that have crossed over from equipment into evidence. The guitar beside the bookcase, tuned once, in October. The telescope purchased after one genuinely spectacular night in a friend's yard, deployed since on zero nights of any kind. The floor loom, which weighs forty pounds, cannot be hidden, and is aware of both facts. These items entered the home as possibility. They now function as furniture with an opinion.

The condition has no clinical name. Petitioners arrive by searching "hobby supplies guilt" and "bought equipment, never used it" — at hours when the equipment is, presumably, asleep — and the Department accepts both phrasings for filing purposes. You bought it in sincerity, and you used it four times, which the mind rounds to never. Sincerity was never a usage schedule — nobody mentions this at the register.

Where Gifts Are Concerned

A significant fraction of the graveyard was donated. Someone supportive heard you mention watercolors — once, in passing, possibly about someone else's watercolors — and by December there was a 36-tube professional set with brushes whose price you have declined to look up. This is the heaviest class of holding the Department processes. The gift was love; the object has since retrained as homework, assigned by the one person you cannot bear to disappoint.

For the record, then, and the record is permanent: they bought you a gift, not a deadline. Anyone who loved you enough to buy the loom will survive its retirement. The Department has processed thousands of these filings and has yet to log a single giver who wanted the guilt included. That part you added yourself, free of charge, which is not like you — everything else here was expensive.

On the Question of the Guitar

Petitioners regularly ask the Department whether they should sell the guitar. The Department is not licensed to advise, having applied for the license some time ago — you understand. It can only report what the files show: nobody manages to sell the guitar while it is still an accusation. Listed in guilt, it stays listed; the photographs come out apologetic and buyers can tell.

Discharge first. Once the file is closed, the guitar reverts to what it always was — spruce, mostly — and may then be sold at a fair price, given to a nephew, or hung on the wall as an instrument that is permitted to be decorative. Museums do this constantly and no one says a word to them.

Territorial Matters

Craft supplies annex territory the way empires do: gradually, then officially. A drawer becomes the yarn drawer. A closet falls without resistance. The guest room retains the title ceremonially, and guests, sleeping beside the kiln, understand their standing. If you have typed "craft supplies taking over" into a search bar, the annexation is complete and you are writing from occupied territory.

The Department does not require you to liberate anything. It requires only that the occupation be entered in the record, whereupon it converts from an active campaign into a historical site. Historical sites can be visited fondly, curated, thinned out at leisure. Some, eventually, are even reopened to guests.

Available Remedy

The instrument is Form UB-1. Four dollars for a Standard Discharge; seven with Full Honors, which adds a gold seal suitable for equipment above a certain price point. State the undertaking — "The Kiln," "Astronomy, Backyard," "Sourdough (Starter Deceased)" — select a Cause of Abandonment from the approved schedule, and receive a numbered entry in the permanent public registry, guilt computed to the day. Those preferring discretion may file WITHHELD: the registry shows a number and keeps its counsel.

The certificate is a satirical novelty document with no legal effect, a status it shares with the exercise bike's warranty card. Refunds within seven days, in full, by reply to your receipt. The object stops being an accusation the moment the paperwork takes over that duty — paperwork was built for it, and unlike the loom, it enjoys the work.

APPROVED CAUSES FOR THIS MATTER: “Would Have Had to Practice” · “The Tutorial Ended” · “January”. The schedule accepts write-ins.

The fee is four dollars. It is the least expensive thing ever to enter this hobby, and the only one that came with closure.
